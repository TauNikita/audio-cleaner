It is just past four in the morning on February 27th, 2014. The center of Simferopol, the capital of Ukraine's Crimean Peninsula, is dark and nearly silent. Inside the building of the Council of Ministers of Crimea, the seat of the peninsula's regional government, a few security guards sit through what they expect to be another quiet night shift. Outside, a small convoy rolls to a stop. Dozens of armed men in green camo step out and move quickly toward the entrance. They carry modern Russian rifles, wear body armor and balaclavas, and move with the calm precision of professional soldiers. But there is one strange detail about them. Their uniforms carry no flags, no insignia, no unit patches, nothing at all to say who they are or which country they serve.
They barricade the doors, stack furniture against the windows, drag in crates of weapons, and raise a Russian flag over the roof. By the time the sun comes up over Simferopol, the elected government of Crimea no longer controls its own house.
For the next several weeks, journalists, governments, and ordinary Ukrainians will argue endlessly about who these silent men are. Locals, half in fear and half in mockery, will call them "the polite people." The world's press will call them "little green men." And the man who sent them, sitting in the Kremlin, will look straight into television cameras and insist, again and again, that they are not his soldiers, that Russia has nothing to do with any of this. He is lying. And a little over a year later, he will admit it on camera himself.
This is the story of how, in roughly one month, Russia tore away a region the size of Belgium from a neighbor it had promised to protect. It did so due to a mix of brilliant deception, overwhelming military pressure, a sham referendum, and an unprecedented political turmoil in Kyiv. This is the story of the annexation of Crimea.
To understand why Crimea fell so fast, we need a little historical background. The peninsula has been fought over for centuries; the Russian Empire annexed the Crimean Khanate in 1783 under Catherine the Great. In May 1944, Stalin deported the entire Crimean Tatar population, the peninsula's indigenous people to Central Asia, where nearly half died of hunger and disease. Their homes were handed to settlers from Russia, which is the main reason that by 2014, ethnic Russians made up somewhere around 60% of Crimea's roughly two million people, with ethnic Ukrainians and the slowly returning Crimean Tatars as large minorities. Then, in 1954, Soviet leader Nikita Khrushchev's government formally transferred Crimea from the Russian Soviet Republic to the Ukrainian Soviet Republic, a move that meant little while both were ruled from Moscow, but everything once the Soviet Union collapsed.
Before the green men appear, it is worth pausing to look at the map of Crimea, because the way the two sides are positioned explains almost everything that follows.
After the Soviet collapse, in 1997, Russia and Ukraine signed a set of agreements that split the Black Sea Fleet and let Russia lease the main base at Sevastopol, along with a string of airfields, such as Kacha and Gvardiiske, ranges, and other coastal sites. At the same time, its troops were capped at no more than 25,000 on the peninsula, with hard limits on how many armored vehicles, artillery pieces, and combat aircraft it could keep there, and, crucially, it was not allowed to bring in new or modernized weapons without Ukraine's consent, or to move its forces around outside the agreed bases. The lease was meant to expire in 2017. But in April 2010, it was extended all the way to 2042. From that moment, Russia quietly began breaking the old rules, slipping modernized and freshly repaired equipment onto the peninsula and steadily reinforcing the units based there.
So what does each side actually have in Crimea by early 2014?
On the Russian side sits the entire Black Sea Fleet, headquartered in Sevastopol, built around its flagship, the missile cruiser Moskva. Around it comes a mix of frigates, smaller missile corvettes and patrol ships, minesweepers, submarines, and, most importantly, a squadron of large landing ships. Its ground component is the 810th Marine Brigade, based right in Sevastopol. And this is the important part: for years before 2014, that brigade has been doing something unusual on its training grounds. It has been helping to prepare and equip exactly the kind of irregular, so-called "self-defense" local paramilitary formations that will later flood the streets. From around 2012, Russia has been intensively building up roughly four brigades' worth of forces in Crimea, close to the cap, manning them with professional contract soldiers and re-arming them with newer equipment.
Against all of this, what can Ukraine put on the peninsula? On paper, somewhere between 18,000 and 22,000 Ukrainian servicemen are stationed there. But the real combat strength is thin, scattered, and, as the next few weeks will show, riddled with men who will not fight. The actual ground component amounts to essentially three units: the 36th Separate Coastal Defense Brigade at Perevalne, with around 1,200 men, and two marine battalions, the 1st at Feodosia and the 501st at Kerch. There are internal security and police formations too, like the roughly 730-strong 47th "Tyhr" Special Purpose Regiment. And by pure chance, one more Ukrainian unit is on the peninsula that does not belong to the permanent garrison at all: a reconnaissance company of the mainland 25th Airborne Brigade, which has come down to Crimea in late February for exercises near Perevalne.
In the air, the 204th Tactical Aviation Brigade at Belbek is on paper the single most capable MiG-29 unit in all of Ukraine, more than 600 personnel and over forty MiG-29 fighters plus a few modernized L-39 trainers, commanded by Colonel Yuliy Mamchur. But years of neglect and sabotage have taken their toll: out of all those jets, only about a dozen are even in flying condition, and of those, just three or four fighters and a single trainer are genuinely fit for combat. The only other flying unit of note is the navy's 10th Naval Aviation Brigade at Saky, operating mostly search-and-rescue helicopters and military transport planes.
And then there is the navy itself. A single ageing frigate as its flagship, 4 corvettes, its only submarine, several minesweepers, a medium and a large landing ship, as well as dozens of smaller patrol and support craft. And where those ships happen to be sitting will shape much of what follows. Most are at Sevastopol: the corvettes Ternopil, Lutsk, and Khmelnytskyi and the command ship Slavutych, in and around Streletska Bay, and the lone submarine Zaporizhzhia in the main Sevastopol Bay. A second group, the 5th Surface Ship Brigade, lies up the west coast at the Southern Naval Base at Novoozerne, on the long sea-lagoon of Lake Donuzlav: the corvette Vinnytsia, the medium landing ship Kirovohrad, the large landing ship Kostiantyn Olshansky, and the minesweepers Chernihiv, Cherkasy, and Henichesk. The flagship frigate, the Hetman Sahaidachny, is the navy's only asset away at sea. Put together, ground, air, and naval components have no chance of defending Crimea, especially because, by Ukraine's own later admission, around half of all its servicemen, including many officers there will end up changing sides, the product of advance arrangements, sky-high bribes, and local commanders who have been quietly promoting treason even before 2014.
And there is a deeper problem with the Ukrainian military overall. By sheer inertia, its best and most ready brigades, its tank, mechanized, and airmobile units, sit in the west and the north of the country, where the old Soviet army had once been postured to face NATO. The south and the Crimean approaches are thinly held, and the Donetsk and Luhansk regions have no regular army brigades at all. On the eve of 2014, in other words, the Ukrainian army is still facing the wrong direction, built to fight an enemy in the west, just as the real one comes at it from the east and the south.
Meanwhile, under the cover of "providing security" for the Sochi Winter Olympics, Moscow has moved heavy airborne units down toward Crimea, including the 31st Air Assault Brigade and the 98th Airborne Division, into the neighboring Krasnodar region just across the strait. Back in November 2013, the Russian airbase at Gvardiiske inside Crimea had suddenly taken delivery of 45 rail tankers of aviation fuel instead of its usual dozen, fuel that will soon feed the transport planes flying in troops. And in the Black Sea, an entire Russian naval squadron, the flagship cruiser Moskva, the frigate Pytlivyy, the small corvettes Alexandrovets and Muromets, the minesweepers Kovrovets and Turbinist, and the reconnaissance ship Priazovye, has put to sea from Sevastopol in early February under the banner of "securing the Olympics." When the Games end on February 23rd, that squadron does not simply go home. It sails to the Russian port of Novorossiysk, takes on board paratroopers and marines with their equipment, and turns back toward Sevastopol to deliver them.
There is one last piece on the board, and it is aimed not at Crimea but at the rest of Ukraine. On February 26th, Putin abruptly orders massive military exercises right up against the Ukrainian border, alerting the 6th and 20th armies of the Western Military District, part of the Central Military District, and the Airborne Troops. Within days the drill involves, by various estimates, up to 150,000 troops, more than 90 aircraft and 120 helicopters, some 880 tanks and over a thousand other vehicles. It is a loaded gun pressed against mainland Ukraine, making it clear that if Kyiv tries to fight for Crimea, a far larger invasion could come crashing across the border. Every decision Ukraine's leadership makes about Crimea now has to be weighed against the risk of losing the entire country.
So, now, to the leadership. For three months, hundreds of thousands of Ukrainians have filled Kyiv's central square, the Maidan, in protest after President Yanukovych abruptly rejected his long-promised agreement to move Ukraine closer to the European Union, choosing closer ties with Russia instead. The demonstrations start peacefully but grow bloodier as the winter of 2014 drags on. Government riot police and unidentified snipers open fire on the crowds. Over a hundred protesters are killed, most of them on February 20th. It is worth noting that Russia has always pushed an alternative story, claiming the protesters somehow shot themselves to frame the government, but the overwhelming evidence points to the security forces, with close ties to Russia.
Instead of curbing protests, the bloodshed fuels them even more which quickly breaks Yanukovych's government. On February 21st, he quietly flees the capital, by helicopter to Crimea, with Russia as his final destination, once again demonstrating who is pulling the strings from the very beginning. On February 22nd, the Ukrainian parliament votes to remove him from power and right away sets the date for new elections. The speaker of parliament, Oleksandr Turchynov, becomes acting president, and Arseniy Yatsenyuk will soon head the new caretaker government. Within days, a Russian Black Sea Fleet landing ship, the Azov, will carry the deposed president out of the marine base at Sevastopol to southern Russia.
But here is the part that matters most for what follows: it is not just the president who runs. Ukraine's entire military and security command effectively collapses and flees east. The defense minister, Pavlo Lebedev, who, remarkably, holds Russian citizenship, disappears into Russia, along with the head of the security service, the interior minister, and the commander of the Internal Troops. The newly installed chief of the General Staff will openly sabotage orders and later defect to occupied Crimea himself. Ukraine suddenly has a brand-new, untested civilian leadership, a half-collapsed army of the average post-Soviet republic, an empty treasury, a police force that has just been shooting its own citizens, and almost no one left at the top of the military to give a coherent order.
In Moscow, Vladimir Putin is watching all of this very closely, and he sees an opportunity. That very day, February 23rd, the Winter Olympics that Russia has hosted in the Black Sea resort of Sochi come to a close. As the world watches the fireworks and the closing ceremony, the orders that will eventually trigger the largest war in Europe since World War 2 are already flying out.
Yet, the first move is not made by soldiers in the open. It is made by crowds, and, quietly, by men who have already arrived from Russia or been trained by Russian marines.
On February 23rd, in Sevastopol, the Russian navy's home port, a rally of around 20,000 people packs the central square. A mix of Crimeans dismayed and confused by the revolution in Kyiv and outright armed separatists and agitators carrying Russian passports quickly acclaim a local businessman named Alexei Chaly as their new "people's mayor."
On that same February 23rd, around 15,000 people gather in Simferopol in support of Ukraine. All because Chaly's acclamation has no legal basis whatsoever, yet by February 24th the Sevastopol city council simply hands him power, a Russian citizen and one of the key figures in the events to come. That same day, Russian armored personnel carriers quietly choke off the road entrances to Sevastopol. The base of the operation is secured.
The decisive clash comes on February 26th. That day, the Crimean parliament is scheduled to meet, and there are strong fears that pro-Russian deputies are preparing to vote for separation from Ukraine, or even to invite Russia in. To stop this, the Mejlis, the representative assembly of the Crimean Tatars, led by Refat Chubarov and the veteran dissident Mustafa Dzhemilev, calls its people into the streets together with other Ukrainians and Russians who oppose annexation, already in sight. Somewhere between 10,000 and 15,000 pro-Ukrainian demonstrators surround the parliament building. Facing them are a few hundred activists of a small pro-Russian Crimean party called, literally, "Russian Unity." The two crowds shove and brawl. In the crush and panic, two people from the pro-Russian side die, one of a heart attack, one trampled, and dozens more are injured. In the chaos, the parliamentary session is cancelled.
The resistance of these civilians forces the Russians to change their plans, because "Russian Unity" simply does not have enough supporters in Simferopol or the other towns to repeat what happened in Sevastopol, filled with way more Russian supporters and saboteurs. So from February 26th, busloads of Kuban Cossacks, paramilitary patriotic formations from Russia's Krasnodar region, begin crossing into Crimea "at the invitation" of local Cossacks. These, together with local separatists, form the bulk of what Russia will call a "spontaneous self-defense force of the Crimean people."
On top of that, the very next morning, the little green men appear at the parliament.
The men who take the two government buildings are Russian special operators. At the core of the assault force are men of Russia's brand-new Special Operations Forces unit "Senezh". A number of them in plain civilian clothes rather than uniform, alongside a reconnaissance-and-sabotage group staged out of Sevastopol from the 45th Separate Spetsnaz Regiment and the 31st Air Assault Brigade. A security camera inside the Council of Ministers building catches the moment almost to the second: at 4:23 a.m., unidentified soldiers arrive, neutralize the guards, take the main entrances, and walk straight into the chairman's office. On both administrative buildings, the Ukrainian flags come down and the Russian ones go up.
They issue no demands. They simply take the buildings and wait. The man running the whole Crimean operation is the SpecOps own commander, Major General Aleksey Dyumin, a former Putin bodyguard and deputy chief of the GRU, the same officer widely credited with organizing Yanukovych's flight to Russia just days earlier.
What happens next inside the occupied parliament is a coup carried out at gunpoint. With the building sealed off and ringed by a "human shield" of pro-Russian "self-defense" volunteers, among whom observers spot a Russian intelligence veteran named Igor Girkin, a man who will be central to many events in the Donbas and in this story, the deputies are summoned. Under the watch of armed men, the parliament "votes" to dismiss the existing Crimean government and to install a new prime minister: Sergey Aksyonov, the 41-year-old leader of that same small "Russian Unity" party, which won only about 4% of the vote in the last election.
The parliament claims dozens of deputies are present, but several will later say their votes were cast for them while they were not even in the room. Girkin himself will openly admit afterward that the gunmen rounded up the deputies and, in his words, forced them to vote.
While this is happening, Crimea is being sealed off from the rest of Ukraine. Armed men set up checkpoints on the narrow strips of land connecting the peninsula to the mainland, the Perekop Isthmus and the Chonhar crossing, manned by Russian troops alongside Cossacks. Ukrainian television channels begin vanishing from the air, replaced by Russian ones.
On February 28th, the operation expands from politics to hard military targets, and the targets are the airfields. The first thing to understand about that morning is that Gvardiiske, the airbase near Simferopol is not a Ukrainian field that has to be captured at all. It is a Black Sea Fleet airbase the Russians already use, which is exactly why it can serve as the front door. As many as eight heavy Il-76 transport planes, the very aircraft that stockpile of aviation fuel had been waiting for, land there, while eleven Mi-24 attack helicopters cross into Crimea straight from Russian airspace. They are full of GRU special forces, among them the 22nd Spetsnaz Brigade, and paratroopers of the 7th Guards Air Assault Division flown up from Novorossiysk, who unload and drive straight out to do the real work: shutting down the airfields that actually are Ukrainian. At the same time, columns of armor, including "Tigr" vehicles the legal Russian garrison never possessed, roll out from Sevastopol and Gvardiiske toward the Crimean capital.
The biggest prize is Simferopol's international airport, the peninsula's main gateway to the world. In the morning of February 28th, around 120 armed men, a mix of fleet troops, FSB officers, and those GRU spetsnaz, backed by a hundred or so Cossacks, roll up in trucks, take over the terminal and the local state broadcaster's building. Near Sevastopol, the Belbek airfield was surrounded the night before: late on February 27th a column of unmarked trucks and armored carriers, several hundred men in all, ringed the base, cut the airmen of the 204th Brigade off from their parked jets, and disabled the last few fighters still able to fly. The same routine plays out across Crimea's smaller fields: Russian troops occupy the old military airstrip at Dzhankoi in the north, and sixteen truckloads of soldiers drive onto the airfield at Kirovske near Feodosia. Out at sea, meanwhile, a Russian missile corvette, reportedly the Tarantul-class Ivanovets, blocks the exit from Balaklava Bay, trapping Ukraine's maritime border guard ships, while special forces surround their base on land. Ten years later, that same Ivanovets will be hunted down and sunk by Ukrainian sea drones in Lake Donuzlav. Over on the east coast, one of the fleet's Bora-class missile hovercraft, the Bora or the Samum, reportedly seals the harbor mouth at Feodosia in the same way.
On March 1st, the events continue to move toward the full and formal annexation at lightning speed. Aksyonov publicly appeals to Vladimir Putin for "help" in keeping "peace" in Crimea, and claims control over all police and military forces on the peninsula. Conveniently, within just a few hours, Russia's upper house of parliament votes unanimously to authorize Putin to use the Russian armed forces in Ukraine.
In Kyiv, the new acting president, Turchynov, declares Aksyonov's appointment illegal and the Federation Council's vote an act of aggression. But here Ukraine faces an agonizing choice, because Russians are already flooding the peninsula, and giving the troops there an order to actively resist would immediately result in bloodshed. Turchynov's government orders Ukrainian forces in Crimea not to open fire and does not redeploy any reinforcements to the peninsula.
It is easy, with hindsight, to call this a catastrophic mistake, and many Ukrainians do. But consider the position Kyiv is in. The army has been gutted and neglected for years and is in no shape to fight; only about 6,000 troops in the whole country are more or less combat-ready. The hardware is no better. Across the entire country the army can field only around 235 aging T-64 tanks of doubtful reliability, a few hundred infantry fighting vehicles and personnel carriers, no more than fifteen serviceable Mi-24 attack helicopters, and barely eight flyable Su-25 jets. Worse, Russia has just launched those enormous exercises along Ukraine's eastern border poised for a much larger invasion.
Ukraine's leaders fear that a single firefight in Crimea, one dead Russian soldier, one burning vehicle, would be exactly the pretext Moscow wants. To blame any bloodshed on the new, not-yet-recognized government in Kyiv, then send those massed troops across the border as "peacekeepers," precisely as Russia did in Georgia in 2008. Such a "peace enforcement" could drive straight on defenseless Kyiv or swallow the entire south and east of Ukraine in one move. The very facelessness of the green men is part of this trap: by refusing to admit they are his, Putin keeps the freedom to escalate into open war if the Ukrainians give him an opening, or to quietly fold the whole operation if they do not. So Kyiv chooses restraint, seeking to avoid escalation and keep the crisis in the political and diplomatic arena.
That restraint is also why Russia's most reckless move goes unchallenged: flying troops straight into the peninsula aboard those Il-76 transports at Gvardiiske, right to the doorstep of Crimea's capital, Simferopol. A gamble that only works because no one fires on the planes. It is, in miniature, the very same plan Russia will try at Hostomel almost exactly eight years later to capture the capital of Ukraine, with much the same kind of airborne troops, including the very 45th Spetsnaz and 31st Air Assault Brigade that are operating in Crimea in 2014. The one Ukrainian unit that might threaten the transports, the 55th Anti-Aircraft Missile Regiment, by then down to just a few dozen men but still holding 36 Buk-M1 launchers, is simply captured by around 30 Russian special forces operators before it can fire a single shot. Forces of the 76th Air Assault Division reach as far as one of the extra bases of the Ukrainian Air Force, Kirovske, also on March 1st. The same happens with elements of the 40th Radiotechnical Brigade spread out across Crimea. Meanwhile, the command of the 174th Anti-Aircraft Missile Brigade operating S-300s simply turns out to be traitors and fully cooperates with the Russians from the very beginning.
On March 1st, Turchynov appoints a new commander for the entire Ukrainian Navy, Rear Admiral Denis Berezovsky. The very next day, March 2nd, Berezovsky walks into the navy's Sevastopol headquarters, surrounded by Russian forces, and swears allegiance to the pro-Russian Crimean authorities. Kyiv immediately dismisses him and opens a treason case, naming Rear Admiral Serhiy Hayduk in his place. Within a few weeks Berezovsky will be made a deputy commander of Russia's own Black Sea Fleet. This kind of treachery, from senior officers all the way down, will become one of the defining features of the fall of Crimea.
While its troops inside Crimea are ordered to hold their fire, Kyiv is not entirely frozen. As Russia locks down the peninsula, the new government scrambles what little it has that can actually move and fight. The first units put on alert are the ones with the highest readiness, the special forces regiments and the airmobile brigades, manned largely by contract soldiers rather than raw conscripts.
Their destination is the Kherson region, the slice of mainland Ukraine directly north of Crimea and the only land gateway on or off the peninsula. From early March, battalions of the 79th Airmobile, the 30th Mechanized, and the elite 95th Airmobile Brigades begin pushing south toward the narrow necks of land at Perekop and Chonhar, to keep the occupation from spilling out onto the mainland.
For a brief moment, Ukraine even considers pushing the other way. There is a plan to move troops into the northern tip of Crimea and grab a foothold in a few towns before Russia fully consolidates its grip. To see whether it is even possible without a fight, an officer of the 79th Brigade, Major Dmytro Marchenko, climbs into a civilian car and simply drives down to Armiansk, the town guarding the northern entrance to the peninsula, to scout it with his own eyes. What he finds ends the idea on the spot: the main road into the town is already under the guns of Russian armored vehicles. The plan is cancelled, and the Ukrainians dig in on the mainland side instead. It is a small, almost unnoticed moment, but remember Marchenko's name, because eight years later, as a general, he will become one of the men who stop the Russian army cold outside Mykolaiv.
What follows over the next three weeks is a series of sieges, conducted base by base and ship by ship, won mostly through overwhelming pressure rather than gunfire, and behind it, a steady tide of Russian reinforcements arriving by sea.
Back inside Crimea, the Ukrainian garrisons can only sit and wait. On March 2nd, Russian troops surround the base of the 36th Separate Coastal Defense Brigade at Perevalne, as well as the marine battalions at Feodosia and Kerch. They demand surrender. The Ukrainians refuse, and a series of standoffs begins that will last for weeks. Not every Ukrainian unit around Perevalne gets encircled, though. The visiting reconnaissance company of the 25th Airborne Brigade, under Lieutenant Colonel Palas, is not tied to any base for the Russians to surround, so instead of waiting behind a fence it stays out of sight, quietly watching the occupiers' movements. On March 3rd, the Russian Black Sea Fleet commander, Admiral Aleksandr Vitko, reportedly delivers a blunt ultimatum to the Ukrainian forces: surrender by five o'clock the next morning, or face an assault on units across the whole of Crimea. The deadline comes and goes. The assault does not happen. That same day, Russian warships pen the corvette Ternopil and the command ship Slavutych inside Sevastopol Bay, and a fleet officer arrives at Belbek with a simple demand for the airmen: come over to the "Crimean authorities." Meanwhile, again and again, the big amphibious vessels, landing ships like the Saratov, the Nikolai Filchenkov, the Novocherkassk, the Yamal, and the Azov, cross from Novorossiysk to Sevastopol, each one unloading hundreds of troops and dozens of vehicles, while Russian troops seize the Kerch ferry crossing in the east, opening a second supply route straight from Russian soil to bring over reinforcements like the 18th Motor Rifle Brigade. By March 7th, Russia has moved around 10,000 troops into Crimea by sea alone.
The blockade produces scenes that show both the worst and the best of those days. At the Sevastopol naval headquarters, the traitor Berezovsky returns, this time with Cossack escorts, trying to talk the assembled Ukrainian officers into following him over to the Russian side. The loyal new commander, Hayduk, reads out the order stripping Berezovsky of his post and announcing treason charges to his face. According to accounts of that day, the officers applaud, and then, standing in their occupied headquarters with Russian forces all around them, they break into the Ukrainian national anthem. At the same time, in the west, near Novofedorivka, the one modest Ukrainian success of those weeks is taking shape at the Saky air base, home to the 10th Naval Aviation Brigade. It too is blockaded, but its acting commander, Ihor Bedzai, decides not to wait for orders that never come. Watched and almost certainly wiretapped, he plans a breakout in secret and times it for one o'clock in the afternoon, when the Russian troops ringing the field tend to relax and break for lunch. On the signal, the brigade's helicopters lift off straight from their hangars, without even taxiing, followed by the fixed-wing aircraft, and in just eighteen minutes four planes and five helicopters are airborne and gone, flown out to the mainland base at Kulbakino near Mykolaiv. About five days later, a pilot in civilian clothes slips back and flies out one more helicopter, the one assigned to the frigate Hetman Sahaidachny. It is the first Ukrainian unit to pull its equipment out of the trap, on nothing but the nerve of its own officers. Eight years later, the 10th Brigade will be named after Bedzai, after he himself is killed in action, shot down by a Russian fighter jet during a search-and-rescue mission.
On March 4th comes one of the most famous images of the whole annexation. At Belbek airbase, the Ukrainian commander, Colonel Yuliy Mamchur, decides he will not just sit behind his fence. He gathers around 50 to 100 of his men of the 204th Tactical Aviation Brigade, has them leave their weapons behind, raises the Ukrainian flag and the old Soviet-era banner of a World War II fighter regiment that the unit traces its honors to, and marches them straight toward the Russian-held part of the airfield. The Russian troops fire warning shots into the air over their heads. The unarmed Ukrainians keep walking. The standoff lasts hours and ends with a tense compromise allowing a small Ukrainian team back to service their aircraft.
Meanwhile, the bulk of Ukraine's ships at the Southern Naval Base are anchored inside Lake Donuzlav, a long bay connected to the sea by a single narrow channel. The flagship cruiser Moskva, trailed by a clutch of support ships, guards the exit. Then, on March 6th, the Russians tow out an old, decommissioned anti-submarine cruiser, the Ochakov, along with a rescue tug, and deliberately sink them across the mouth of the channel. With these blocking the fairway, the ships of the 5th Surface Ship Brigade, the corvette Vinnytsia, the landing ships Kirovohrad and Kostiantyn Olshansky, and the minesweepers Chernihiv, Cherkasy, and Henichesk, are now bottled up inside the bay, unable to reach the open sea; their crews pull them off the piers and gather them in the middle of the lagoon, out of boarding reach. And presiding over the whole blockade is the missile cruiser Moskva, a ship that, eight years later, will meet a famous end of its own in the continuation of this very war. For now, though, the Moskva enjoys a position of prison guard near Donuzlav.
Russian troops, Cossacks, and "self-defense" squads work through the peninsula's smaller military objects almost like a checklist: the arms depot at Inkerman, a missile unit and a base around Bakhchisarai and Fiolent, border posts at Chornomorske, Massandra, and Hurzuf, and, on March 14th, the storming of a Ukrainian foreign-intelligence facility at Alushta.
With Crimea militarily locked down, Russia turns to manufacturing a vote that could give the whole operation a facade of democratic legitimacy. This political track has been running in parallel with the sieges all along.
Go back to March 6th, then. Even as those standoffs drag on across the peninsula, the occupied Crimean parliament moves the referendum dramatically forward, to March 16th, and changes the question. The timing itself shows how fast Russia is improvising: the vote had first been set for May 25th, then pushed to March 30th, and now to March 16th, barely ten days to organize a "referendum" on changing the country a region belongs to. Even the question itself is rigged from the start. Voters are given only two options: either Crimea joins Russia, or Crimea returns to a 1992 constitution that would grant it near-total independence from Ukraine, but with Russian puppet Aksionov as president. There is no option to simply keep things as they are, to remain a normal part of Ukraine.
On March 16th, 2014, the referendum is held, under military occupation, with foreign soldiers patrolling the streets and no genuine international observers present. The official result is comical: Crimean authorities claim that 96.77% have voted to join Russia, on a turnout of around 83%.
But here is the part that exposes the lie, and it comes from Russia's own side. Putin's personal advisory body, the Presidential Human Rights Council, will later publish a report after its members visit Crimea, and its findings quietly demolish the official numbers. According to that report, real turnout is likely somewhere between 30 and 50%, not 83%, and of those who do vote, only around 50 to 60% choose to join Russia. Do the math, and it means that perhaps only around a quarter, maybe even less, of Crimea's total population actually votes for annexation, and they do so literally at gunpoint.
The next day, March 17th, Crimea formally declares independence from Ukraine and immediately asks to be admitted into the Russian Federation. The West responds with its first, fairly cautious round of sanctions. Moscow, on the cusp of success, shrugs it off.
March 18th, 2014, is the day Russia makes it official, and the day the blood is finally spilled.
In Moscow, Vladimir Putin rises to address a packed assembly of Russia's political elite. The speech is triumphant. When he finishes, he signs the treaty making Crimea and Sevastopol part of Russia, standing alongside the men who have handed it to him: Aksyonov, the Crimean parliament speaker Vladimir Konstantinov, and the Sevastopol "people's mayor" Alexei Chaly.
While the champagne is poured there, the first soldier dies in Simferopol. That afternoon, at a Ukrainian military facility, a topographic and photo-mapping center that has been surrounded for days, around 15 masked gunmen storm the base. In the shooting, a 36-year-old Ukrainian warrant officer named Serhiy Kokurin is shot dead in a watchtower. A fellow officer, Captain Valentyn Fedun, is wounded. In the same chaotic raid, a man on the attacking side, a Cossack volunteer from the "self-defense" forces named Ruslan Kazakov, is also killed. The assault is reportedly led by the unit of that same Russian FSB officer, Igor Girkin. Serhiy Kokurin is widely regarded as the first Ukrainian soldier killed in what will become the long Russo-Ukrainian war.
The death of Kokurin finally changes Kyiv's orders. Turchynov authorizes Ukrainian troops in Crimea to use their weapons to defend their own lives. But it is far too late to change the outcome.
On March 19th, Russian forces storm and seize the Ukrainian Navy headquarters in Sevastopol itself, and the loyal commander Hayduk is briefly taken prisoner before being released. On March 20th, Russia's lower house of parliament ratifies the annexation in a vote of 445 to 1, the single lonely "no" coming from a deputy named Ilya Ponomarev, who will later flee Russia. The very same day, Russian forces capture the Ukrainian corvettes Ternopil and Lutsk at Sevastopol, and storm a third, the Khmelnytskyi, in the one boarding where shots are actually fired. On March 21st, the Federation Council ratifies the treaty as well, and Putin signs the final law.
But across Crimea, dozens of Ukrainian bases and ships are still flying the blue-and-yellow flag, their crews refusing to surrender to a country that, until weeks earlier, had been a close ally. So in the final week of March, the so-called "polite people" stop being polite. They come in to take the holdouts by force, and tellingly, Russia saves its elite Special Operations Forces, the same kind of troops who took the parliament, for the hardest jobs, leaving the rest to the "self-defense" militias and less prestigious units.
On March 22nd, Russian troops finally storm the Belbek base. Around five in the evening a BTR rams the fence, and Russian special forces pour in from the airfield side, hurling stun grenades at the lightly armed defenders, who by now have barely a rifle for every five men. Colonel Mamchur is not even there to defend his men; he has been lured out to "negotiations" at the fleet headquarters and quietly arrested. The same day, the Russians capture the single most unusual prize in the Ukrainian fleet: the submarine Zaporizhzhia, moored in Sevastopol Bay, Ukraine's only submarine, with a crew of 78 under Captain First Rank Oleh Orlov. The crew splits down the middle, about half, including the captain, choose to leave and stay loyal to Ukraine, while the other half defect and join the Russian navy. Around this time, Russia claims it has raised its own naval flag over the great majority of Ukraine's warships and the Russian flag over well over a hundred captured military sites.
On March 24th, the brutal end comes for the marines at Feodosia, on Crimea's eastern coast. There, the men of Ukraine's 1st Separate Marine Battalion have refused every demand to surrender. So Russia sends in its special operators, storming the base with two Mi-24 attack helicopters overhead and a ground assault using stun grenades. After a burst of hand-to-hand fighting, around 80 marines are overpowered and captured. Their commander, Lieutenant Colonel Dmytro Delyatytskyi, and his deputy are abducted and held for several days. The story of these marines captures the whole tragedy of Crimea in a single number: of roughly 600 men in that battalion, only 137 will ultimately choose to leave the peninsula and keep serving Ukraine, including Delyatytskyi himself, who during the full-scale invasion will rise to command the entire Ukrainian Marine Corps. The rest stay, switch sides, or simply go home. Around the same days, the long siege of the 36th Brigade at Perevalne ends too, and there as well, the majority of the men choose Russia over withdrawal.
That same March 24th, with the situation hopeless and his soldiers being overrun base by base, acting President Turchynov finally orders the full withdrawal of all Ukrainian forces and their families from Crimea. The fight, such as it is, is over.
Almost. Out at Lake Donuzlav, two ships will not give up. When the commander of the 5th Surface Ship Brigade defects on March 20th and orders his ships to the piers to surrender, the medium landing ship Kirovohrad and several others do just that, their captains going over to Russia. But the big landing ship Kostiantyn Olshansky and the minesweeper Cherkasy refuse. Their crews re-raise the Ukrainian flag, pull back out to the middle of the lagoon, and hold a circular defense there for days. On March 24th, the same day as the withdrawal order, around 200 Russian assault troops storm the Olshansky with grenades and automatic weapons; only about twenty of her crew are still aboard. That leaves the Cherkasy, commanded by Yuriy Fedash, alone. Rather than surrender, her crew try repeatedly to ram and maneuver their way out of the bay, dodging Russian fire and even Russian helicopters, in a stubborn, almost hopeless bid for the open sea. They try again on March 25th. In the end it is no use: with two Mi-35 helicopter gunships overhead and three assault boats closing in, Russian forces storm and seize the Cherkasy, lowering the last Ukrainian flag flying over a warship in Crimea.
Not everyone leaves empty-handed, though. When the withdrawal order comes, that visiting reconnaissance company of the 25th Airborne Brigade, the one that has spent the occupation slipping through the chaos near Perevalne rather than sitting trapped in a base, simply forms up and drives off the peninsula to the mainland, taking out every vehicle, weapon, and round it brought in. It is one of the very few Ukrainian units to leave Crimea completely intact.
On April 6th, after the base at Saky has fallen to the Russians, one of the 10th Brigade's pilots, Major Stanislav Karachevsky, is shot dead at point-blank range by a Russian soldier in the officers' dormitory at Novofedorivka, among the last lives taken in the seizure of Crimea.
When it was all tallied, the damage to Ukraine's navy was devastating, by some estimates, around three-quarters of the entire fleet was lost in Crimea. The flagship frigate Hetman Sahaidachny only escaped because, by sheer luck, it had been away at sea returning from an anti-piracy mission off the coast of Somalia, and it sailed home to the mainland port of Odesa instead of into the trap. And what Russia later returned, it returned on its own terms, as a gutted, mocking fraction of what it had taken. Under an agreement reached in April, the captured warships were towed out to neutral water in batches and sailed on to Odesa, around thirty-five vessels in all, mostly the older and the damaged, before Russia simply halted the process as the war in the Donbas escalated. The 204th Brigade's jets were trucked out of Belbek to Mykolaiv that spring, but the Russians kept the best, most modernized fighters for themselves and handed back the rest disassembled and gutted, each airframe stripped of as many as a dozen key components. Ukrainian engineers spent months cannibalizing and rebuilding them; by that summer, a handful of those same MiG-29s were already flying combat missions over the Donbas. The land equipment mostly never came back at all, the Feodosia marines alone had left around sixty BTR-80 armored carriers sitting on their base when it was stormed, and most of Ukraine's armor in Crimea simply changed owners.
So how did it happen? How did a nuclear-armed neighbor steal an entire region of a country of 40 million people in about a month, killing only a handful of men in the process?
Militarily, the answer is a combination of three things. First, position: Russia already had a powerful, legal military presence inside Crimea thanks to the Sevastopol fleet base, so its first soldiers didn't have to invade at all, they just had to walk out of their barracks without their insignia, while the fleet and transport planes quietly ferried in at least 10,000 more reinforcements. Second, surprise and deception: by disguising regular troops as "local self-defense" and flatly denying everything, Russia kept Ukraine and the West paralyzed, arguing about what was even happening, and anxious to avoid the bloodshed that might ignite something far bigger, while the operation was already finished. And third, and most painfully for Ukraine, betrayal. From the navy commander Berezovsky on his first day in the job, to the majority of the marines at Feodosia, to the thousands of soldiers and police across the peninsula, an enormous share of Ukraine's own forces in Crimea simply switched sides or laid down their arms. Combined with the indecision of Kyiv, this meant Russia met almost no resistance. The "little green men" were good. But, as 2022 will show, they were good only against an enemy with tied hands.
On March 27th, 2014, the United Nations General Assembly passed Resolution 68/262, declaring the Crimean referendum invalid and affirming Ukraine's territorial integrity, by a vote of 100 in favor to just 11 against. Russia was suspended from the elite club of major economies, the G8, which simply became the G7 again. Sanctions began to bite and would only grow. And the whole affair exposed a bitter betrayal that still haunts the world today: back in 1994, in the Budapest Memorandum, Ukraine had given up the world's third-largest nuclear arsenal in exchange for written promises from Russia, the United States, and Britain to respect its borders. In 2014, Russia tore that promise to shreds, and the lesson the rest of the world quietly absorbed was that giving up your nuclear weapons can get you invaded. On the other hand, it was also Ukraine that bore responsibility for the corruption, shortsightedness, and negligence that had ruined its military before 2014 and allowed Crimea to be filled with outright Russian agents. But that failure was grounded in good intentions, too, the wish to be a peaceful, free, and democratic country that kept not just friendly but brotherly ties with all its neighbors, and that truly believed in the power of international law. This bet did not pay off.
As a military operation, the annexation of Crimea was almost flawless: fast, cheap, nearly bloodless, and it cemented Putin's image at home as the heroic, unbeatable "gatherer of Russian lands." And that is exactly where the future catastrophe was born. Because Moscow looked at how easily Crimea had fallen and drew a fatal, arrogant conclusion: that the rest of Ukraine was just as weak, just as divided, just as ready to crumble at the first push of disguised soldiers and engineered crowds.
Within just a few weeks, they would test it. That same Russian FSB operative, Igor Girkin, the man who had forced the Crimean deputies to vote and helped storm the base where Serhiy Kokurin died, would slip across the border into eastern Ukraine, into the Donbas, with a small armed group, and briefly seize the town of Sloviansk. Even some of the soldiers were the same: the 45th Spetsnaz regiment with the support of the 31st Air Assault Brigade that had taken the Crimean parliament turned up again as the armed core of the seizures in the Donbas.
And eight years later, the Kremlin would make the very same bet one final time, on the grandest scale of all. Once again, the plan assumed the Ukrainian state would simply collapse, that the government would flee or switch sides, the army would melt away, and a relatively small, elite force could decapitate the whole country before it could react. Once again, the spearhead was the airborne spetsnaz, the 45th Spetsnaz Brigade, the very same kind of troops who had walked into the Crimean parliament. On the first morning of the full-scale invasion, only a few hundred of them stormed Hostomel airport on the doorstep of Kyiv by helicopters, planning to seize a runway and open an air bridge of Il-76 transports, the same kind of planes, flying the same kind of mission, as the ones that had carried troops to Gvardiiske in 2014. Behind them, long thin columns of armor raced for Kyiv and other large cities, expecting to be waved through, not shot at. It was the Crimea playbook scaled up to an entire nation: move fast, move light, expect the defenders to fold right away or simply block them in their bases until they do. But 2022 was not 2014. The hollow, leaderless army that had surrendered Crimea without a fight had spent eight years becoming something else entirely, and at Hostomel, the airborne assault that was supposed to win the war in a matter of days was instead shattered in a matter of hours.
In other words, the annexation of Crimea had cost Russia almost nothing. Believing the rest of Ukraine could be taken the very same way would end up costing it almost everything.
